Scenarios for a future climate

The future climate depends on the quantity of greenhouse gasses that will have been emitted by the human society using fossil fuels as a source of energy since the start of the industrial revolution. Since this level depends on how society responds globally to limiting the use of fossil fuels to reduce the change in the climate, the future state of the climate is represented as scenarios, known as Representative Concentration Pathways.

Global Climate Models

The impact of the change in greenhouse gas concentration on the climate is quantified using sophisticated Global Climate Models on some of the largest super computers in the world to solve the differential equations that govern the state of the atmosphere over the globe and for 100 years into the future. The Global Climate Models are not perfect representations of the processes that drive our climate and the United Nations runs the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change to assess the results from about 50 models that have been used to model a range of scenarios. The data used in this dashboard is based on the results from set of 8 models that have been further processed to increase their resolution over Australia.

The likely climate for the various scenarios is given as a range, rather than as a single number, due to this uncertainty in the modelling.

Fire Danger Index

Uncontrolled bushfires have been a part of the Australian experience for thousands of years due to the dry climate and the combustible eucalypt forests. The intensity of a fire depends on the fuel (amount, and dryness) that is available and the weather (temperature, humidity, and wind speed) at the time of the fire. The amount of fuel on the forest floor will increase during wet years, and then dry out during a prolonged drought, so the risk of uncontrolled bushfires will likely change as the temperature increases and the rainfall patterns change.

The McArthur Forest Fire Danger Index (FFDI) combines the past rainfall and evaporation with temperature, humidity, and windspeed to provide a summary index of the risk that is presented by a bushfire should one ignite.
